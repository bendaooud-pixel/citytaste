# Marrakech Like a Local: The Insider Guide Most Travellers Never Read

I have lost count of how many times I have walked into Marrakech expecting chaos and walked out with a notebook full of small, perfect moments. The city rewards people who slow down. After years of returning — sometimes for a week, sometimes for a month — I have learned that the difference between a stressful trip and an unforgettable one comes down to a handful of decisions most guidebooks gloss over. Here is what I actually tell friends before they go.

## Where to Stay (and Where Not To)

Everyone wants a riad inside the medina, and you should have one — sleeping behind those thick walls with a fountain courtyard is part of the experience. But location matters more than star ratings.

### Choose the Right Medina Pocket

Aim for a riad near **Riad Zitoun el Kedim** or the **Kasbah** area rather than deep behind Jemaa el-Fnaa. The narrow lanes near the main square get loud until 1am and the touts are relentless. A riad in the Kasbah, by contrast, is a five-minute walk to the Saadian Tombs and Bahia Palace, yet quiet enough to sleep with the windows open. Expect to pay 600–900 MAD (about €55–€85) per night for a beautiful mid-range riad with breakfast included.

### Always Arrange the Pickup

Riads sit on lanes too narrow for cars. A taxi can only drop you at the nearest derb (alley entrance), and then a porter with a cart wheels your bags in. Ask your riad to send someone — most do it free or for 20 MAD. Trying to find the door yourself at night, dragging a suitcase past "helpful" strangers who then demand 100 MAD, is the classic first-night mistake.

## Getting Around Without the Stress

The medina is walkable end to end in 25 minutes, so you rarely need transport inside it. For longer trips, the **petit taxis** (beige) are cheap if you insist on the meter — a ride from the medina to Gueliz should be 20–30 MAD. Drivers will often refuse the meter for tourists; just say "compteur, s'il vous plaît" firmly, and if they decline, wave the next one down. There is always another.

For day trips to the Atlas Mountains or Essaouira, I book a shared grand taxi or a private driver through my riad. A full day with a driver to the **Ourika Valley** (about 60 km southeast) runs 600–800 MAD for the whole car, not per person — worth splitting with others.

## The Things Most Guides Miss

This is where Marrakech really opens up.

### Go to the Square Twice

**Jemaa el-Fnaa** is a tourist cliché by day and genuinely magical by night. Skip it at noon when it's just orange juice stalls (still grab one — 5 MAD). Return at dusk when the food carts fire up, the storytellers gather, and the Gnaoua musicians play. Eat at stall 14 or any cart crowded with Moroccans, not the ones with English menus. A plate of grilled merguez, bread, and harira soup costs around 40–50 MAD.

### The Hidden Rooftops

Some of the best views aren't at paid viewpoints but at café rooftops. **Café des Épices** and **Nomad** in the spice square (Rahba Kedima) let you nurse a mint tea (15–25 MAD) above the rooftops at golden hour. Go around 5:30pm before the crowds.

### Learn to Read the Souks

The souks are organised by trade — dyers in one lane, metalworkers in another, leather near Bab Debbagh and the tanneries. Knowing this turns wandering into navigation. When you get lost (you will), walk toward the sound of motorbikes; that leads to a main artery. If a "guide" insists a lane is closed, it isn't — that's the oldest trick to redirect you to a shop where he earns commission.

## Eating Beyond the Tagine

Yes, eat tagine — but the real local food is broader. Find a **mechoui** stall in the medina for slow-roasted lamb (around 80 MAD for a generous portion). Try **tanjia**, a Marrakchi specialty cooked for hours in clay pots — ask for it at **Mechoui Alley** off the square. For breakfast, a **msemen** (flaky flatbread) with honey from a street stall costs 5 MAD and beats most hotel buffets.

### Haggling Honestly

Bargaining isn't a fight; it's a conversation. Start at roughly 40% of the asking price, smile, and be willing to walk — that often halves the price instantly. For a quality leather pouf, expect to settle around 200–350 MAD.

## Final Thoughts

Marrakech overwhelms the people who rush it and embraces the people who linger. Give yourself three full days minimum, accept that you'll get lost, and treat every cup of mint tea as an invitation to slow down. Do that, and the city stops being a postcard and starts feeling like somewhere you belong.
